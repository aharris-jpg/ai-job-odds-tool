The biggest surprise in the data is that some of the jobs people assume are safest because they’re skilled or white-collar actually rank among the most exposed, while many physical, in-person roles remain far harder for AI to replace.

Key Findings

Desk-based digital work dominates the top.

Computer Programmers lead at 45%, followed by Customer Service Representatives at 42% and Data Entry Keyers at 40%.

Documentation-heavy work is highly exposed.

Medical Records Specialists rank around 40% because these roles revolve around organizing structured information.

Communications roles appear surprisingly exposed.

Market Research Analysts and Public Relations Specialists both rank above 25% due to writing and research overlap.

Healthcare shows a major split.

Administrative healthcare jobs rank high, while clinical roles like nurses rank closer to 4%.

Physical work appears far safer.

Jobs like bartenders, electricians, and firefighters sit near 0% because they require real-world activity.

Low odds doesn’t mean zero AI impact.

Even safer roles will still see AI tools assist tasks inside the job.
